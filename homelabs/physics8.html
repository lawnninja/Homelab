Introduction to electronics: Paralell and series connections

Theoretical notes: All materials are made up from atoms, and all atoms consist of protons, neutrons and electrons. Protons have a positive electrical charge. Neutrons have no electrical charge (that is they are Neutral), while Electrons have a negative electrical charge. Atoms are bound together by powerful forces of attraction existing between the atoms nucleus and the electrons in its outer shell. When these protons, neutrons and electrons are together within the atom they are happy and stable. But if we separate them from each other they want to reform and start to exert a potential of attraction called a potential difference.
Now if we create a closed circuit these loose electrons will start to move and drift back to the protons due to their attraction creating a flow of electrons. This flow of electrons is called an electrical current. The electrons do no flow freely through the circuit as the material they move through creates a restriction to the electron flow. This restriction is called resistance. Besides the resistance, the electric current is defined by another two physical measures: Voltage and Current. The voltage is defined as the work done by the current over the electric charge, representing the potential of the circuit. The current is the number of charges that pass through a conductor in a fixed amount of time and could be thought of as some kind of "velocity" of the charges.


The electric component whose job is to limit the current that passes through a conductor is called a resistor. Because of the properties of the electric current, it behaves differently depending on the way the resistors are connected. There are two types of resistor connections: parallel connection and series connection. We say two or more resistors are connected in series when the same current passes through all the resistors.
